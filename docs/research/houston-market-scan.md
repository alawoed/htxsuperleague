# Houston Market Scan

**Status:** Working research  
**Research date:** 2026-07-15  
**Purpose:** Inform validation; not a substitute for direct customer interviews or quoted vendor costs

## Market signals

Houston is a plausible market for a focused soccer venture:

- The U.S. Census Bureau estimates Houston's 2025 population at **2,397,315**, up **4.2%** from the 2020 estimate base. The city is **44.2% Hispanic or Latino** and **29.3% foreign-born**. This supports a culturally broad soccer audience and the recommendation to test bilingual service, but demographics alone do not prove willingness to pay. Source: [U.S. Census Bureau QuickFacts](https://www.census.gov/quickfacts/fact/table/houstoncitytexas/PST045225).
- Multiple operators advertise current small-sided soccer in Houston, including 5v5, 6v6, 7v7, 8v8, and 9v9. This demonstrates active supply and likely demand, but it also means HTX Super League enters a competitive market.
- Houston-area options span recreational social leagues, facility-run leagues, sanctioned adult associations, and tournament operators. HTX Super League must choose a clear level and customer promise.

## Venue observations

- The [Houston Parks and Recreation 2026 field fee schedule](https://www.houstontx.gov/parks/pdfs/FeeScheduleBallfields.pdf) publishes comparatively low hourly figures for public fields, but it distinguishes youth and adult use, field levels, and time of day. Availability, permits, field condition, lighting, goals, exclusivity, rainout control, and commercial-league eligibility require written confirmation.
- [Houston Sports Park](https://www.houstonsportspark.com/field-reservations/pricing/) requires inquiries for general pricing, may charge operational fees, and states that certified athletic trainers are required for most contact sports. Venue quotes must include every required service, deposit, cancellation term, and tax/fee.
- Harris County precinct and independent facility reservation systems have different lead times, restrictions, and payment requirements. A low posted hourly price is not a usable budget until the exact field and operating permission are confirmed.

## Sanctioning, officials, and safety observations

- TSSAS states that affiliation with USASA can provide consistent rules, an appeals system, liability and medical insurance, referee training/evaluation, suspension visibility, and tournament access. HTX should request current written costs, coverage documents, obligations, and small-sided approval before deciding. Source: [Texas State Soccer Association-South](https://app.teampass.com/Texas_State_Soccer_Association_South/).
- USASA describes liability coverage for premises and operations of affiliated adult soccer organizations and sanctioned activities. The actual policy, exclusions, limits, deductibles, participant accident coverage, and additional-insured process must be reviewed with a broker or association. Source: [USASA liability insurance](https://usadultsoccer.com/liability-insurance/).
- U.S. Soccer maintains an updated referee-abuse prevention policy and education program. HTX should build sanctions and captain behavior requirements around applicable governing rules and a zero-tolerance culture. Source: [Respect the Call](https://www.ussoccer.com/rap).
- The National Weather Service recommends a designated lightning monitor who is not occupied as a coach or referee. The CDC recommends limiting or rescheduling outdoor activity during the hottest parts of the day and acting immediately on heat-illness symptoms. Sources: [NWS lightning and outdoor sports](https://www.weather.gov/safety/lightning-sports) and [CDC heat and athletes](https://www.cdc.gov/heat-health/risk-factors/heat-and-athletes.html).

## Opportunity gaps to validate

These are hypotheses, not findings:

1. Captains will pay a modest premium for reliable schedules, fields, officiating, and communication.
2. Players want a more visible competition identity, verified statistics, high-quality photos/highlights, and awards.
3. Existing leagues leave gaps in transparency around discipline, rescheduling, refunds, and customer support.
4. Sponsors want a measurable connection to Houston's adult soccer communities but lack packaged local inventory.
5. One high-quality 9v9 venue can become a recognizable weekly destination.
6. English/Spanish critical information will improve trust and conversion.

## Evidence still required

- 25 structured interviews with captains from different Houston areas and competition levels;
- at least 10 player interviews, including women and coed participants;
- at least 5 venue quotes and site inspections;
- referee assignor, crew availability, and fee quotes;
- insurance and sanctioning proposals with actual terms;
- sponsor interviews and at least 3 letters of interest;
- landing-page conversion and refundable-deposit tests;
- travel-time mapping for likely teams;
- seasonality research covering heat, rain, holidays, and competing leagues;
- trademark, entity-name, domain, and social-handle clearance.

## Research conclusion

The concept is credible enough to validate and narrow. It is not yet ready for a venue contract, public pricing promise, or software build. The strongest initial wedge is a professionally presented, digitally supported 9v9 pilot for one clearly defined adult segment. Venue economics and operational quality—not the website—will determine whether the first season works.
