# Growth and Expansion Plan

**Status:** Draft  
**Decision owner:** Founders

## Growth principle

HTX Super League should scale one proven unit at a time. Growth is a replication decision, not a reaction to general interest. The first season should reveal whether the venture owns a repeatable competition and customer experience—not merely whether founders can force one season to work.

## Expansion order

### Horizon 1: Repeat the core

Goal: Run a second season of the same 9v9 product with improved margins, retention, operations, and product reliability.

Requirements:

- at least 6 of 8 pilot teams intend to return and enough teams actually renew;
- venue and official supply remain reliable;
- all critical safety, integrity, and data issues are resolved;
- base economics support the approved price and sponsor requirement;
- founder workload is converted into sustainable roles and processes.

### Horizon 2: Deepen Houston 9v9

Options, tested separately:

- add a second ability division with promotion/relegation planned for the following cycle;
- add a women's, coed, or over-30 competition;
- run a standalone cup or one-day/two-day tournament;
- add free-agent assessment and team placement;
- expand media, awards, and sponsor activations;
- add a second weekly night at the same venue.

Select only one major new complexity per cycle unless staff, field, officials, and systems already support both.

### Horizon 3: Expand the Houston footprint

- second venue cluster serving a demonstrated geographic audience;
- inter-division cup or championship event;
- venue-partner operating model;
- trained matchday managers and referee coverage by cluster;
- sponsor packages that distinguish local and league-wide inventory.

### Horizon 4: New formats and locations

- 7v7, 11v11, futsal, corporate, or seasonal tournament products;
- another Texas location using a documented playbook;
- native mobile applications if repeat usage and device capabilities justify them;
- licensing or operator partnerships only after quality, IP, data, and governance controls are mature.

Youth competition is a separate operating model, not a simple age filter. It requires dedicated safeguarding, guardian consent, background checks, communications, medical authorization, scheduling, insurance, staffing, and legal readiness.

## Expansion scorecard

| Dimension | Minimum evidence before expansion |
|---|---|
| Demand | Qualified waitlist large enough to fill the new product without weakening the core |
| Retention | Core renewal target achieved with actual payments, not only survey intent |
| Delivery | At least 95% service-standard performance and policy-compliant resolution of all matches |
| Safety/integrity | No unresolved critical incidents; high risks treated and accepted |
| Economics | Positive or approved contribution with quote-based expansion budget |
| Venue | Contracted capacity plus weather/backup plan |
| Officials | Assignor confirms sufficient qualified coverage and backups |
| People | Named, trained owner for each new operating unit |
| Product | Competition configuration supports expansion without manual control failure |
| Partners | Sponsor/venue obligations remain deliverable and brand-safe |

## Growth loops

### Team loop

Reliable experience → captain referral → qualified team pipeline → stronger divisions → higher retention.

### Content loop

Real matches → quality stories and highlights → player/team sharing → relevant audience growth → sponsor value.

### Partner loop

Professional fulfillment → venue/sponsor trust → better terms and referrals → improved experience and economics.

### Product loop

Operational data → identified friction → focused product improvement → lower admin cost and fewer errors → capacity for growth.

## Product evolution

| Evidence | Product response |
|---|---|
| Captains repeatedly fail to collect player information | Improve invite, reminders, team-funded registration, and readiness UX |
| Matchday connectivity is unreliable | Add offline-tolerant check-in/reporting or a stronger controlled fallback |
| Statistics are trusted and heavily used | Expand verified event capture and profiles |
| Spectator traffic becomes recurrent | Add richer following, notifications, media, and venue experience |
| Users repeatedly need device-native capabilities | Evaluate native apps after defining the specific unmet job |
| Multiple competitions create configuration friction | Invest in competition templates and scoped administration |

Do not build a mobile app because mobile apps are expected. Build one only when native notification, camera, offline, wallet, or other device capabilities produce measurable value beyond the responsive web platform.

## Organization evolution

### Pilot

Founder-led commissioner with part-time functional owners and critical contractors.

### Repeat seasons

Dedicated league operations manager, clearer registrar/support coverage, retained referee assignor, finance process, and repeatable matchday contractor pool.

### Multi-division / multi-venue

Head of competition, venue-cluster matchday managers, centralized registration/support, commercial manager, content operations, and accountable product/technology ownership.

Founder governance should move toward goals, capital allocation, risk, senior appointments, and expansion approval rather than weekly manual administration.

## Financial growth rules

- Measure contribution by competition, team, match, and field hour.
- Separate recurring operating cost from one-time product and brand investment.
- Do not use new-team deposits to conceal prior-season obligations.
- Set a working-capital reserve for refunds, weather, claims, and vendor deposits.
- Require sponsor cash assumptions to be backed by contracts and collection timing.
- Price each new format from its own field, official, safety, staffing, and customer-acquisition economics.

## Geographic expansion playbook

Before announcing a new location:

1. identify a local operator accountable for delivery;
2. validate a concentrated team pipeline and travel pattern;
3. secure venue and officials with backups;
4. review local contracts, permits, insurance, taxes, policies, and emergency services;
5. configure localized sponsor inventory and communications;
6. train and certify the operating playbook;
7. run one controlled pilot;
8. compare performance to the Houston core before continuing.

## Long-term strategic options

If the core is proven, HTX Super League can become:

- a recognized Houston competition portfolio;
- a tournament and event operator;
- a media and community platform around local soccer;
- a venue operating partner;
- a software-enabled league operator in additional locations;
- a blueprint for future sports ventures created through TRIMNDS.

The venture should preserve strategic choice. It should not declare a nationwide platform ambition before the Houston unit is repeatable.
