# League Format and Competition Framework

**Status:** Draft planning baseline  
**Decision owner:** Competition lead  
**Approval required:** Founders, venue, officials/assignor, insurer or sanctioning body, and legal reviewer as applicable

This is not the final rulebook. It defines the competition product to be validated and the decisions required to create the binding season regulations.

## Recommended pilot format

| Element | Planning baseline |
|---|---|
| Participants | Adult 18+; proposed men's/open division pending validation |
| Teams | 8 |
| Divisions | 1 |
| On-field format | 9v9 including goalkeeper |
| Registered roster | 12–16 players |
| Matchday roster | Proposed maximum 14; confirm with pricing and rules |
| Regular season | 8 matches per team |
| Playoffs | Top 4; semifinals and final |
| Matchdays | Approximately 10, including playoffs and awards |
| Match length | Proposed 2 × 35 minutes with controlled halftime and turnover |
| Points | 3 win, 1 draw, 0 loss |
| Laws | Current IFAB Laws as modified by published HTX 9v9 local rules and governing-body requirements |
| Venue model | One venue, or a tightly controlled two-venue cluster |

With eight teams, eight regular-season matches require 32 matches. A top-four semifinal and final add 3 matches, producing a 35-match pilot. The competition lead must produce a fixture model that is fair despite the eighth match extending beyond a single round robin.

## Scheduling principles

- Publish the full regular-season schedule before registration closes or no later than the approved schedule-service date.
- Prefer a consistent weekly night and venue.
- Avoid midday summer windows; use the approved heat and weather policy.
- Build at least one recovery/rain date into the facility agreement.
- Do not schedule the same team in the earliest or latest slot disproportionately.
- Define religious holiday, major local event, and venue-conflict treatment before fixture generation.
- Limit captain-requested reschedules and require an objective process, notice period, fees, and opponent/venue availability.
- Treat the public schedule as the system of record.

## Match rules requiring approval

The final rules must state, without ambiguity:

- field and goal dimensions;
- permitted footwear and required protective equipment;
- ball size and who supplies match balls;
- minimum players to start and continue;
- match duration, halftime, added time, and late-start treatment;
- offside application;
- substitution type, location, and re-entry;
- goalkeeper distribution restrictions, if any;
- sin-bin or temporary-dismissal rules, if authorized;
- yellow-card accumulation and red-card consequences;
- team-uniform standards and clash resolution;
- technical area and spectator conduct;
- check-in, identity, roster lock, guest player, and ineligible-player rules;
- forfeits, abandonment, protests, and replay authority;
- regular-season and playoff tie procedures;
- standings tiebreak order;
- stat correction window and official-record priority.

## Recommended competition-integrity controls

### Eligibility

- Each player has one verified identity and date of birth.
- Every player accepts the current waiver and applicable policies before appearing.
- A player may be registered to only one team in a division unless a published exception applies.
- Roster additions close on a stated date before playoffs.
- Guest players, if permitted at all, are regular-season-only and must be registered, insured, eligible, and visible to opponents.
- The league may request government-issued ID at check-in, but should not retain unnecessary ID images.

### Standings

Recommended tiebreak sequence:

1. points;
2. head-to-head points where the tied group permits a fair comparison;
3. goal difference, with consideration of a per-match cap to discourage running up scores;
4. goals scored;
5. fair-play record;
6. playoff or drawing method stated in advance.

The exact algorithm must be approved, tested with multi-team ties, and published before the first match.

### Results and statistics

- The referee's match report is the primary record for score, cards, and incidents.
- Team managers may confirm or dispute within a defined window.
- Only authorized administrators can finalize or amend official results.
- Every amendment requires a reason and audit log.
- Do not publish detailed player statistics that cannot be captured consistently.

## Discipline and appeals model

- Use a published code of conduct and sanction matrix.
- Adopt zero tolerance for threats, discriminatory conduct, fighting, and referee abuse.
- Require captains to control benches and spectators connected to their teams.
- A send-off creates an automatic interim suspension pending review.
- Separate fact gathering, charging, decision, and appeal where staffing allows.
- Publish evidence rules, deadlines, fees, decision authority, and available outcomes.
- Refund an appeal fee when the appeal materially succeeds.
- Do not allow personal relationships, sponsor status, or competitive importance to affect outcomes.

The final policy must align with applicable U.S. Soccer, USASA/TSSAS, venue, and insurer requirements. U.S. Soccer's [Referee Abuse Prevention Policy](https://www.ussoccer.com/rap) should inform the protection standard.

## Awards and recognition

Pilot awards may include:

- league champion and finalist;
- regular-season leader, if distinguished from playoff champion;
- golden boot, only if goal attribution is consistently verified;
- goalkeeper recognition, using a defined method rather than goals-against alone;
- fair play award;
- team of the season, only with a transparent selection process;
- referee or community recognition that does not compromise independence.

Avoid cash prizes in the pilot unless the financial, legal, tax, insurance, competitive-behavior, and promotional implications are reviewed.

## Competition expansion rules

Do not add a second division, new format, or new venue during the pilot. After the season, expansion requires:

- enough qualified teams to fill the new product without weakening the existing division;
- a proven officials and field supply;
- revised budget and staffing;
- approved rules and safety review;
- software support for the added complexity;
- a clear reason that is stronger than general interest.
