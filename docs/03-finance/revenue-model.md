# Revenue Model and Financial Assumptions

**Status:** Draft scenario model  
**Decision owner:** Finance lead  
**Important:** Replace all planning ranges with written quotes before the commit gate

## Financial objective

The pilot should validate repeatable unit economics while staying within a founder-approved cash-loss limit. Break-even is desirable but not required if the founders intentionally fund a capped learning investment. Unplanned losses are not acceptable.

## Revenue model

### Core revenue

- team entry fee;
- player registration or membership fee, if separately stated and justified;
- tournament entry fees in later phases;
- individual/free-agent placement after the team model is stable.

### Commercial revenue

- season and presenting sponsorships;
- matchday, content, award, and statistics sponsorships;
- venue and community partnerships;
- approved vendor fees or revenue share;
- merchandise margin;
- photography or media packages where consent and fulfillment are controlled.

### Deferred revenue

- spectator tickets;
- subscriptions for ordinary competition information;
- data licensing;
- betting, gaming, or alcohol-led activations;
- franchise or expansion fees;
- player transfer or representation fees.

## Recommended participant price structure to test

Use one of two transparent models in interviews:

### Model A: all-in team price

- Team pays one amount covering a stated roster size, officials, fields, playoffs, and standard media.
- Advantages: simple sale and captain budgeting.
- Risks: captain carries collection burden; marginal roster and insurance costs can be hidden.

### Model B: team entry plus player registration

- Example validation range: **$1,750–$1,950 team fee** plus **$25–$40 per registered player**.
- At 14 players, effective team cost is approximately **$2,100–$2,510**.
- Advantages: players accept waivers and pay their own league registration; variable costs align with roster size.
- Risks: more checkout friction and potential incomplete rosters.

Do not publish a fixed price until venue, officials, medical coverage, insurance/sanctioning, payment fees, taxes, equipment, staffing, and contingency are quoted.

## Break-even method

Calculate break-even using cash contribution, not total accounting revenue:

```text
required participant revenue
= total cash costs
- committed cash sponsorship
- conservative other cash revenue

required effective team price
= required participant revenue / paid teams
```

Run the model at 6, 7, 8, and 10 teams. Six teams may reduce match count but does not proportionally reduce insurance, legal, marketing, equipment, or some venue obligations.

## Cash-flow controls

- No non-refundable venue commitment before the minimum team/deposit gate unless founders explicitly fund the risk.
- Separate refundable team reservation deposits from earned registration revenue in reporting.
- Publish when deposits become non-refundable and what happens if HTX cancels.
- Collect the final balance before the schedule or roster-lock milestone.
- Require purchase approval above a defined threshold and two-person approval for refunds outside policy.
- Reconcile payment processor, bank, registration, refunds, discounts, and accounting records weekly.
- Never store card details.
- Record restricted sponsor funds and in-kind value separately.
- Pay referees and contractors on a documented schedule with tax classification reviewed.

## Discount policy

Allow only published, coded discounts with an owner, budget, eligibility rule, and expiry. Recommended types:

- early commitment;
- returning team in later seasons;
- qualified referral;
- sponsor-funded community access;
- hardship support through a controlled application.

Do not negotiate hidden team prices that create distrust or discrimination risk.

## Financial metrics

- cash collected and outstanding by team;
- refund and chargeback rate;
- revenue per team and player;
- direct cost per match and field hour;
- contribution per team;
- sponsor cash collected versus contracted;
- customer acquisition cost per paid team;
- forecast-to-actual variance;
- working capital and worst-case cancellation exposure;
- renewal revenue and retention in later seasons.

## Decisions required before pricing

1. Is the pilot expected to break even or operate within an approved learning loss?
2. What maximum cash exposure may be committed before registrations are fully paid?
3. Which costs are included in the team price?
4. Are player registration and processing fees displayed separately?
5. What is the minimum viable team count and cancellation date?
6. How are weather losses, venue failures, team withdrawal, and force majeure handled?
7. Are prizes cash, in-kind, or recognition only?
8. Which founder can approve spend, discounts, credits, and refunds, and at what thresholds?
